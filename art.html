← To curve a triangle

Turvangles are 16 different interlocking asymmetrical shapes that let users construct imaginative spatial environments. Adding a shape to the structure (nearly) always increases the total number of open connection points, creating organic, branching plant-like forms.

A shape's connection points are either parallel or angled 60° or 120° from one another. This means that while Turvangles do connect to form perpendicular planes, the angles within guarantee plenty of diagonal action.

Fun fact: there are over 80 trillion trillion mathematically unique structures that can be made using all 16 Turvangles.

Note taker, note maker→

Blending instrument and program, this programmable music instrument allows users to physically compose and record a melody by arranging tuned aluminum rods around a circular platform.

Users can "playtest" their composition (and execute their program) by turning the small gear. As the large gear rotates, each note sounds as it comes in contact with the hanging metal chime.

← Ooh, taste 'E'!

Synesthesia is the neurological condition that links two types of sensory experiences together. Most commonly, letters and numbers are automatically associated with specific colors (called grapheme-color synesthesia). But many variations exist - synesthetes may "hear" colors, "see" days and months spatially, or "taste" the sound of a word.

For those of us without extrasensory powers, this device tells you exactly what words taste like! Each letter comes with its own distinct flavor, so all you have to do is pump any word into your cup and drink up. Careful, the taste of "chocolate" may be different than you expect.

Sound insight →

In this three-dimensional music visualization, notes are represented by circular arcs that attach tangentially to one another. Each new measure begins at the same place, letting you visually experience the structural relationships between measures.

Because the relationships we "hear" in music are determined by the ratios between note frequencies, these same ratios were used to determine the diameters of the circles. To account for rhythm, the duration of a note was used to determine its arc's length.
