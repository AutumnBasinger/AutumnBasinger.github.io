Exploring perceptual connections between scent and emotion

We know that scent, emotion, and memory are closely intertwined. But the language used to describe the experience of scent is often ambiguous.

This made me wonder: Could I create scents that accurately conveyed specific emotions?

Roles
Scent designer, exhibit designer

Scent Design

I started out by mixing together food items, body products, plant matter, and whatever else I could find to create scents for eight emotionally-descriptive words:

Wonder, Boredom, Anxiety, Passion, Spontaneity, Contemplation, Satisfaction, and Spite.

During the process, I noticed myself often using words such as dark, high, bright, soft, or sharp to describe the scent I was going for.

Spontaneity, true to its name, wasn't part of my original plan. I had been going for "creativity," a low, sparkly, evenly-distributed scent. But I could never quite capture it, so I renamed the mix. Spontaneity smells to me a bit higher up, slightly off-center, and its sparkles are more attention-seeking.

Exploring Perceptions

Knowing that my mixtures were certainly influenced by my own personal associations, I wanted to see how others would interpret the scents I had created.

In hindsight, I should've first had participants describe the emotion before knowing the ingredients or my intention.

Later, when I no longer had the mixtures, I read the ingredients to a friend and asked what emotions he thought they smelled like. These were his answers:

Wonder - Positivity
Boredom - Studious
Passion - Sensuality
Spontaneity - Excitement
Satisfaction - Comfort
Contemplation - My favorite, because he thought for a while but couldn't decide!
Spite - Hate
